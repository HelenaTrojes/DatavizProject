A Deadly Pattern That Persists

Every year, hundreds of people in the United States are fatally shot by police. While the totals fluctuate slightly, the overall number remains alarmingly consistent — averaging around 1,000 victims annually since 2015.

The number of lives lost reflects not just the scale of the problem, but its unsettling consistency — a reminder that reform remains elusive.

Black Americans are killed at a much higher rate than White Americans

Although White Americans represent the largest share of police shooting victims in absolute numbers,

Black Americans are disproportionately affected when adjusted for population size.

This chart shows fatal police shootings per million residents per year, grouped by race. The height of each bar reflects the risk of being killed by police, while the width reflects that group's share of the U.S. population.

Black Americans are killed at more than twice the rate of White Americans — despite being a much smaller share of the population. Hispanic Americans also face elevated risks. These disparities highlight broader questions of systemic inequality and policing practices in the U.S.

Most Victims Are Young

More than half of the people shot and killed by police are between 20 and 40 years old.

An overwhelming majority of the victims are male - 95%! This striking imbalance raises questions about why young men, in particular, are so frequently involved in fatal encounters. It points toward deeper social, economic, and cultural factors, including how masculinity, risk, policing, and structural disadvantage intersect in high-stakes situations.

What Were Victims Reported to Be Armed With?

In many fatal police shootings, the person killed was reported to be holding a weapon — most often a firearm. Based on 7,729 cases with known weapon data, each of the 77 icons below represents approximately 100 individuals, offering a simplified visual snapshot of the reported circumstances.

*Other includes items such as a taser, hammer, shovel, or blunt objects.

The fact that most victims were reported to be holding a firearm highlights the very real danger officers may face in these encounters. In situations involving visible weapons, decisions are often made in a matter of seconds — with little margin for error. But not all cases are as clear. As we’ll see next, perceived threat isn’t just about what someone holds — it’s also shaped by factors like mental illness, behavior, and uncertainty.

Mental Illness and Threat Level

How often are shootings linked to mental illness or reported threat level?

Although most victims — whether showing signs of mental illness or not — were labeled as posing an "attack" threat, the similar proportions suggest that mental health status had little influence on how threatening individuals were perceived in these fatal encounters. This could indicate that officers responded similarly regardless of visible mental health conditions, raising questions about how threat assessments are made in high-stress situations.
