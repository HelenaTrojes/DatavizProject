A Deadly Pattern That Persists

Every year, hundreds of people in the United States are fatally shot by police. While the totals fluctuate slightly, the overall number remains alarmingly consistent — averaging around 1,000 victims annually since 2015.

The number of lives lost reflects not just the scale of the problem, but its unsettling consistency — a reminder that reform remains elusive.

Black Americans are killed at a much higher rate than White Americans

Although White Americans represent the largest share of police shooting victims in absolute numbers,

Black Americans are disproportionately affected when adjusted for population size.

This chart shows fatal police shootings per million residents per year, grouped by race. The height of each bar reflects the risk of being killed by police, while the width reflects that group's share of the U.S. population.

Black Americans are killed at more than twice the rate of White Americans — despite being a much smaller share of the population. Hispanic Americans also face elevated risks. These disparities highlight broader questions of systemic inequality and policing practices in the U.S.
